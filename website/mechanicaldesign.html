What is a flywheel?

A flywheel is a device that stores kinetic energy in its rotational motion. This kinetic energy, the energy of motion, is different from other energy storage devices like batteries. Batteries store energy chemically, through charged ions in a chemical called an electrolyte. The flywheel has two main components: a heavy disk that spins at a high rotational speed and a motor that allows energy to be added or removed from the disk through changing its rotational speed.

The physics behind a flywheel

Fundamentally, a flywheel is governed by the equation E = ½*I*W2, where I is the moment of inertia of the flywheel, and W is the angular speed. In the case of a cylindrical flywheel rotating around its central axis, the moment of inertia is given by the equation I=½ MR2, where M is the mass of the cylinder, and R is the radius of the cylinder.

Sizing our flywheel

Our initial goal for the project was to create a flywheel that can store 15 Whr of energy (roughly equivalent to an iPhone 11 Pro Max battery). However, we had a few key constraints to navigate. Firstly, the radius of the flywheel was determined by the size of the available stock. To save money, the team decided to use a cylindrical low carbon steel blank from the shop's donation section with a diameter of 15.24cm instead of purchasing new stock. Our second limiting factor was RPM. Early on in the project proposal process, the teaching team expressed a desire that we keep the rotational speed of the flywheel relatively low for safety reasons. Because of this, we settled on a max rotational speed of 6000 RPM (200pi rad/sec). Many industrial flywheels have a thinner central disk and then a thicker outer section to reduce weight while minimally impacting the moment of inertia. We ultimately decided not to go this route since weight reduction was not a priority for our design. Given these constraints, we saw that a flywheel with a radius of 7.25cm and a height of 5.75cm would need to spin at nearly 21500 RPM to reach our energy target. We therefore scaled back our energy target to 0.75 Whr.
For our final dimensions, we settled on a radius of 7.25cm, which is about a ⅓ centimeter less than our stock radius. From this radius, we determined that a height of 5.75cm and a rotational speed of 5000 RPM would meet our energy target. However, given that we wanted the flywheel to spin continuously (rather than spin up and then come to a complete stop), we decided that our effective energy capacity should only be 80% of our total energy capacity. This means that our flywheel's max rotational speed is increased to 5600 RPM, and cycles between ~5600 RPM to ~2700 RPM when outputting energy.
Note: After measuring and weighing our finished flywheel, we updated our moment of inertia and found that the flywheel now theoretically stores 0.77Whr of energy at 5600rpm.

Shaft design

When designing the flywheel, we decided that the shaft and disk would be separate components that would be joined together after machining. We settled on using a shrink fit to join the components as it allowed for relatively good concentricity and minimal additional fabrication. Bearings and Bearing Blocks

To join our shaft and bearings, we decided to press fit them together. The fabrication section of this website contains more information about this process. The bearings were sized to meet our radial loads and to accommodate our planned RPM range. The bearing at the top of the shaft is a radial ball bearing designed to take radial loads, while our bottom bearing is a tapered roller bearing that takes both radial and thrust loads.

Weldment and Top Plate Design

To hold the bearing blocks, we designed a weldment using 1.25x1.25x0.07in square tube that would act as both a containment structure and a mounting point for the motor, electronics and sensing suite.

The weldment is sized such that the flywheel can be lifted off of its bearing block, rotated and then removed between the weldment through two of the side faces. The other sides of the weldment are designed to be too small for the flywheel to fit through, thus aiding in containment in the case of a failure. A top plate is bolted to the topmost face of the weldment. It serves as the mounting point for the motor and the uppermost bearing block.

The top plate has a hole in its center to allow for shaft passthrough, and 4 holes spaced 90 degrees apart around the central hole. These 4 holes are for mounting the motor mount and uppermost bearing block, and are oversized in diameter. This allows users to move the upper bearing block in the XY plane to accurately align the bearings. Furthermore, the motor mount is designed such that the fasteners passing through the bearing block can also pass through the motor mount mounting holes. This allows the user to lock the relative movement of the motor mount and the bearing block to reduce alignment time. The motor and flywheel shaft are joined using a flexible shaft coupler to allow for misalignment. The motor mount is designed to hold the motor at the correct height for shaft coupling. The motor mount is made from 3D printed plastic as the hope is it would act as a sacrificial part to save the motor should an expected failure occur.

Loss Estimation and Constant Power in Testing

During the initial stages of this project, we hoped to model our expected bearing losses and aerodynamics losses. When diving into bearing losses, we found that they depend on a multitude of factors (SKF has an excellent handbook on estimating bearing losses), and calculating them was not the best use of our time. We found a similar issue with calculating the aerodynamic losses. Since the disk is spinning and there is no frontal area facing the flow, it isn’t possible to use a traditional first order approximation for calculating the drag force (such as ½ Cd A v2 p). We were able to find an academic paper regarding flywheel modeling though it did not yield any useful first order approximations for drag. The next step would have been to model the flywheel in CFD, but it was ultimately decided that the team's resources were better spent elsewhere. Since we were unable to calculate any approximations for the losses that we would see in the system, we instead experimentally determined the losses at a range of rotational speeds. There are many factors that make our data inconsistent between runs (such as changing shaft alignment during flywheel reinstallation), but we believe it is a good start. In the graph below, you can find the amount of power that it takes to sustain a given RPM. At 5500 RPM, we need approximately 76W of power to stay at a constant speed. This indicates that there is significant loss in our system. It is important to note that this loss is measured from the input power, and thus represents losses across the electrical system, motor and any mechanical losses.
